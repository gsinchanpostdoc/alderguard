[P6] Abstract
[P7] Black alder (Alnus glutinosa), the phyllophagous leaf beetle (Agelastica alni), the koinobiont larval endoparasitoid (Meigenia mutabilis), and generalist passeriform birds constitute an ecoepidemic system across alluvial forests in Europe. Phyllophagy by beetle larvae causes foliar biomass loss in the tree. The endoparasitoid parasitises susceptible beetle larvae by ovipositing on their integument; emergence of adult parasitoids kills parasitised larvae, providing natural regulation of defoliation. Generalist passeriform birds, following optimal foraging strategies, also contribute to biocontrol by preferentially consuming the most abundant prey during outbreaks. Induced phytochemical changes in pest-infected trees provide stand-level resistance that may accumulate and persist into the subsequent year. Thus, resistance differs across intra-annual and interannual timescales, while vernal regrowth, providing system resilience via canopy recovery, is observable only at an annual timescale. This temporal discordance introduces complexity in system stability. This study uses a process-based hybrid seasonal–annual model of this focal ecoepidemic system to quantify how resistance and resilience jointly structure stability across nine phenological scenarios associated with climatic variation, and applies optimal control theory to contrast three pest-management strategies. Results show that the coexistence equilibrium is the least frequently stable regime across phenological parameter space, while the parasitoid-free state is the most frequent. When all phenological parameters vary simultaneously, the probability of regime shift from coexistence reaches 81.5%. Among the three management strategies tested, only the fully integrated strategy combining parasitoid augmentation, targeted larval removal, and bird-habitat enhancement achieves a locally stable managed equilibrium and does so at minimum cost. These findings indicate that maintaining parasitoid-based biological control requires active, multi-component intervention and that single-agent strategies, though capable of short-term suppression, fail to ensure long-term system stability.

[P9] Introduction
[P10] Black alder (Alnus glutinosa) dominates European riparian zones and supports many flora and fauna habitats (FFH; European Commission, 1992). Outbreaks of the phyllophagous leaf beetle Agelastica alni can substantially defoliate the canopy (Dolch & Tscharntke, 2000). If defoliation recurs, repeated outbreaks may cause forest system collapse due to its vulnerability to other environmental perturbations (Valor et al., 2020). When beetles attack, black alder shows induced physiological changes that may offer stand-level resistance (Dolch & Tscharntke, 2000). Two natural beetle enemies also help regulate their population. One is Meigenia mutabilis, a koinobiont larval endoparasitoid that parasitises beetle larvae (Dolch & Tscharntke, 2000; Stireman et al., 2006). Evidence of specialist birds controlling leaf beetle directly is rare in the literature. However, optimal foraging theory suggests generalist passeriform birds in these forests will feed on the beetles during outbreaks, targeting the most abundant resource (Remmel et al., 2011; Monteagudo et al., 2023). Although empirical evidence suggests reflushing of leaves after defoliation (Dolch & Tscharntke, 2000), repeated outbreaks can potentially constrain the recovery of the whole canopy at the forest level; therefore, it is expected to occur mostly through vernal growth during the next spring following the outbreak season. To prevent canopy loss from the beetle, often, Black alder-dominated habitats restoration involves management intervention, such as beetle removal and parasitoid introduction. The top-down control via optimal foraging by generalist passeriform birds and seasonal growth of each trophic level of this system provide a natural regulation that forms an ecological core of this system (Remmel et al., 2011; Monteagudo et al., 2023). The conversion from susceptible to parasitised beetle larvae forms the epidemic core of the system (Stireman et al., 2006). Overall, this ecoepidemic system offers an opportunity to investigate how natural regulation and stand-level resistance contribute to the system's stability, coupled with resilience from canopy recovery. Understanding the adaptability in this stability from dynamic resistance and resilience can offer insight into optimising the intervention strategies in a changing environment.
[P11] This leaf beetle typically causes outbreaks in spring and early summer. During this period, newly flushed alder foliage provides abundant food for larval feeding and development (Sivrikaya et al., 2020). In response, alder leaves undergo induced phytochemical and physical changes, including increased phenolic content, reduced starch, and the development of traits that deter beetles. These defences determine stand-level resistance and help prevent beetle spread to other susceptible trees (Tscharntke et al., 2001; Dolch & Tscharntke, 2000). At the same time, the koinobiont larval endoparasitoid tracks beetle larval aggregations and parasitises susceptible larvae by ovipositing on their integument. The subsequent emergence of adult parasitoids results in the death of parasitised larvae (Dolch & Tscharntke, 2000; Stireman et al., 2006). Notably, there is no well-studied defence from beetle larvae to the parasitoid. However, beetles possess chemical defences, such as foul odours, that deter specialist birds (Pasteels et al., 1995). While specialist birds rarely forage on this beetle, larvae remain vulnerable to generalist passeriform birds, which select the most abundant prey based on minimal search time (Stephens & Krebs, 1986). All these phenological scenarios relate to climatic variation and human interventions.
[P12] There are at least nine well-documented variable phenologies in this system. The length of the spring–summer season, which changes under a warming climate, determines the vernal growth period of the tree (Renner & Zohner, 2018). This also sets the vulnerability window when beetle larvae are exposed to parasitoids. Vernal growth of black alder controls canopy recovery from past defoliation and supports annual resilience (Dolch & Tscharntke, 2000). Early spring can cause faster recovery and lead to higher resilience. Winter severity influences the overwintering survival of both beetle and parasitoid (Sivrikaya et al., 2020; Stireman et al., 2006). This, in turn, determines the initial density of the susceptible beetle population. It also affects the initial density of parasitoids that can lay eggs and kill beetles, which together determine effective outbreak intensity for the following season. Parasitoid development rate varies with temperature and climate (Stireman et al., 2006). As a result, the mortality rate of parasitised beetle larvae can increase, while the mortality rate of susceptible larvae may decrease with a warmer climate. Aggregate predation pressure on the beetle from generalist passeriform birds also varies widely (Monteagudo et al., 2023). This depends on habitat structure, breeding disturbance, bird community composition, learned avoidance of beetle odour, and bird-habitat enhancement. Foliage growth changes due to accumulated phytochemical defence in frequently attacked trees (Tscharntke et al., 2001; Dolch & Tscharntke, 2000). This can lead to lower carrying capacity for foraging aggregation of beetles. Decreasing foraging aggregation can lead to less parasitoid attraction toward beetles, and thus a lower parasitoid attack rate (Stireman et al., 2006). Therefore, differential variability in multiple phenologies within the same system can produce complex and adaptive system stability via dynamic resistance and resilience.
[P13] The adaptive stability from the variable phenologies can offer strategic planning in habitat restoration and especially pest control intervention. The optimal control theory can help minimise the cost of intervention strategies using the interdependence of these phenologies, intervention measures, and environmental changes (Lenhart & Workman, 2007). However, in order to find the optimal strategies of pest management intervention, subject to phenological scenarios, understanding possible stable equilibria and their desirability is also necessary. For instance, since there is no direct empirical evidence on the behavioural or visual distinction of the parasitised beetles from susceptible ones, the consumption of both classes can be assumed to be equal by generalist passeriform birds (Segoli et al., 2024). Under this assumption, bird predation on parasitised larvae can be interpreted as intraguild predation; foraging on parasitised beetle larvae reduces future recruitment of parasitoid population by killing them with beetle larvae before their emergence (Polis et al., 1989; Rosenheim, 1998; Fakhry et al., 2022). In a theoretical scenario of the absence of parasitoids, the generalist passeriform birds can still provide natural regulation of the beetle, but only during the outbreak, increasing the risk of canopy loss. Therefore, even if that theoretical equilibrium is stable, it is not desirable. Therefore, this research focuses on optimising intervention strategies that maintain coexistence equilibria and detect tipping points to shift to any other possible equilibria of the system.
[P14]  Despite detailed empirical investigation into phenologies in this focal ecoepidemic system, the canopy state regime and adaptive stability remain poorly understood. Measuring resistance is challenging and may only provide a limited understanding of the current stage. For instance, it is empirically difficult to quantify the exact thresholds for plant chemical accumulation, required bird diversity, or parasitoid density needed to control the beetle population without risking extinction. In this study, we measure resistance as a loss from the expected outbreak intensity across seasonal and annual scales. We define resilience as the reciprocal of the rate of recovery in annual carrying capacity. However, following Van Meerbeek et al. (2021), we view ecological stability as a multidimensional property rather than a single measure. This perspective helps place our focal system within a broader stability framework that includes resistance, resilience, and the ability of the system to remain within the same domain of attraction after perturbation (Donohue et al., 2016; Hillebrand et al., 2018). Here, we use that framework only as a conceptual guide. The actual stability measures are defined using biologically relevant variables from our hybrid seasonal–annual model: low larval density and low cumulative defoliation indicate strong resistance during the outbreak season, whereas rapid return of annual carrying capacity and system state toward the reference annual equilibrium indicates resilience across years. In this way, Van Meerbeek et al. (2021) provide the general language of stability, while our model provides the system-specific quantities used to evaluate the objectives.
[P15] Using these definitions, we set three aims to advance the understanding of adaptive stability in this ecoepidemic system. This approach can support management planning under optimal control theory. O1. We identify the most probable stable equilibria of the system across all phenological scenarios. To meet this aim, we form a process-based model that captures phenologies and intervention strategies through parameters. We represent variability in phenologies via parameter space. We then search for all possible stable equilibria in this variable parameter space. Next, we test H1: under all variability in phenologies, the system is likely to converge to a desirable coexistence equilibrium, defined by the presence of a canopy, bird, and parasitoid that can prevent future beetle outbreaks if reintroduced. O2. We quantify the probability of the system shifting to an undesirable equilibrium from its current coexistence equilibrium, unless coexistence is the likeliest equilibrium across parameter space. In that case, we test H2: changes in certain phenologies, or parameter values, may serve as early warning signals of a shift to an undesirable equilibrium that still maintains stability. If so, we aim to classify them for each potential undesirable equilibrium. O3. We determine the minimal effort needed for three pest control strategies against the leaf beetle to prevent the system from shifting regimes across parameter space. Using optimal control, we aim to minimise the cost of effort per strategy. To guide intervention planning, we test three sets of scenarios, ranging from the least costly to the most costly strategy combinations, against all variable phenologies.
[P16] Methodology
[P17] We use the structural property of the systems' well-studied ecological interactions to model the system using process-based modelling. Then we characterise the system's stability across parameter space in this study with emphasis on persistence conditions, equilibrium transitions, and comparative control performance. Therefore, we employ a four-stage analysis to characterise stability from the structural properties of the system within a unified seasonal–annual framework. First, we formulate a set of process-based within-season ordinary differential equation models and nest them within an annual recurrence process. Second, we identify equilibria as fixed points of the annual process and derive the basic reproduction rate of the parasitoid at the parasitoid-free fixed point using the next-generation matrix with modifications required for the hybrid seasonal-annual structure following van den Driessche & Watmough (2002). Third, we analyse the local stability of the annual fixed points through the Jacobian of the annual process, including computation of the Jacobian by means of variational (sensitivity) equations of the within-season flow, together with identification of bifurcation boundaries in parameter space (Kuznetsov, 2004). We define the parameter space based on eigenvalues of the annual-process Jacobian crossing the unit circle. Fourth, we formulate an optimal control problem and solve it numerically through Pontryagin's Maximum Principle and the forward–backward sweep method to test the stability of the system in light of optimal control theory (Lenhart & Workman, 2007). We then perform a systematic robustness evaluation across the full plausible parameter ranges in the numerical analyses (see Section 2.3) and compare the resulting optimal-control trajectories and stability landscapes across the alternative control scenarios introduced in the Introduction. We organise the methodology into three subsections: (1) model formulation, (2) structural stability analyses, and (3) numerical analyses. The model formulation includes an explicit mapping of nine phenological scenarios to model parameters (Table S1 in Annex 1). The structural stability analyses derive equilibrium conditions, the parasitoid invasion threshold, bifurcation boundaries, and optimal control solutions. The numerical analyses evaluate all stability and control outcomes across the full plausible parameter space defined by these phenological scenarios (Annex 1). Together, the three subsections address: (O1) identification of the most probable stable equilibria across phenological scenarios, (O2) quantification of regime shift probability and identification of parametric drivers of instability that may translate to candidate early warning signals, and (O3) determination of the minimal intervention effort under three pest-management scenarios.
[P18] 2.1 Model formulation 
[P19] The model comprises two coupled modules that operate at distinct temporal scales. Module 1 is a within-season eco-epidemic ordinary differential equation system that characterises the tri-trophic dynamics during the spring-summer larval vulnerability window of the leaf beetle. Module 2 is an annual discrete recurrence process that connects successive seasons by updating the beetle recruitment cohort, the parasitoid overwintering population, and the implicit alder foliage carrying capacity according to cumulative defoliation damage. All state variables are expressed per hectare of alluvial forest stand. Because resistance emerges within and across seasons, while resilience depends on annual canopy recovery and carrying-capacity return, only a two-timescale model can identify equilibria, regime shifts, and minimal interventions reliably.

[P46] 2.3 Stability Analyses
[P47] The long-term stability of this system is determined by the annual recurrence map rather than by the within-season ordinary differential equation (ODE) considered in isolation. The within-season ODE (Equations 1-4) characterises loss and accumulation processes over a finite interval τ ∈ [0, T]. Setting  produces within-season pseudo-equilibria, which do not represent ecologically relevant steady states, since the system is reset annually through recruitment, overwinter survival, and foliage recovery equations (5-8). Multi-year dynamics, including outbreak recurrence, parasitoid persistence, and tipping risk, are governed by the annual map. The stability analysis is analytically well-posed but does not admit a closed-form solution. Specifically, the annual map G and its structure, existence conditions for fixed points, the Jacobian  of the annual map (derived via the chain rule and variational equations), the parasitoid invasion threshold  as the dominant eigenvalue of , local stability criteria (spectral radius less than one), and bifurcation conditions (eigenvalue crossing the unit circle) are all derived analytically. In contrast, the seasonal flow  and its derivatives are evaluated numerically, as the Holling Type II functional responses render the within-season ODE nonlinear and analytically intractable. This approach, which combines analytic stability conditions with numerical evaluation of the seasonal flow, is standard and mathematically rigorous for hybrid eco-epidemic models (Kot, 2001; Kuznetsov, 2004). The analysis proceeds through a 10-step framework: 1. Definition the annual state and the seasonal flow operator, 2. Construction of the annual recurrence map 3. Identification of fixed points (annual equilibria) of the map 4. Assessing local stability of annual fixed points via the Jacobian of the annual map 5. Derivation of the seasonal flow via variational (sensitivity) equations. 6. Calculation of Parasitoid invasion threshold from the annual map 7. Identification of tipping points as bifurcation boundaries of the annual map. 8. Assessing Optimal control via Pontryagin's Maximum Principle and the forward-backwards sweep method 9. Searching Tipping under optimal control 10. Assessing robustness under parameter uncertainty. The detailed calculation of these steps is in Annex 2, and has been generated via Mathematica, MatchCad, and manual calculation.
[P48] The stability analysis addresses the three study objectives through a stepwise analytical structure. For Objective 1 (O1), all ecologically distinct fixed points of the annual map are identified (Step 3). Their local stability is classified using the Jacobian eigenvalues (Step 4). The parasitoid invasion threshold  is then computed (Step 6). The analysis scans the full parameter space to determine the frequency of each equilibrium type across phenological scenarios. The equilibrium with the highest frequency is designated as the most probable equilibrium. Objective 2 (O2) is addressed by tracking eigenvalue magnitudes and bifurcation boundaries across parameter space (Step 7). The regime shift probability is computed as the fraction of sampled parameter sets converging to an alternative equilibrium. Parameters are ranked by their influence on bifurcation proximity using the PRCC of the dominant eigenvalue magnitude (Step 10). Parameters identified as drivers of instability become candidate early warning signals if the corresponding phenology is empirically observable and its variation matches the destabilising parameter direction. Objective 3 (O3) is addressed by optimal control analysis (Step 8) and a three-scenario comparison (Step 9). This compares resistance, resilience, stability, and cost outcomes across parasitoid-only, combined parasitoid-and-bird, and full integrated control, applying a lexicographic decision rule: feasibility first, then minimum cost.
[P49] 2.4 Definitions of resistance, resilience, and tipping during stability analyses 
[P50] In this study, the framework of Van Meerbeek et al. (2021) is used to structure the interpretation of stability, not to replace the model-specific metrics required by the present eco-epidemic system. Their synthesis treats ecological stability as a set of complementary properties that describe how a system responds to perturbation relative to a reference condition and its domain of attraction. We therefore map those general properties onto the quantities generated by our annual map. Resistance is represented by suppression of outbreak intensity, measured through equilibrium defoliation and peak larval load; resilience is represented by the rate of return to the annual equilibrium, measured through the dominant eigenvalue magnitude and return time; and tipping is evaluated from loss of local stability and from ecologically relevant functional thresholds. The parasitoid invasion threshold is included as a complementary regime-structure metric for Objective 2, rather than as a direct Van Meerbeek stability component.
[P51]  We operationalise the first two components for the hybrid seasonal-annual model as follows. All metrics are standardised against a reference state and bounded between −1 and 1, enabling comparison across phenological scenarios. We apply these standardised metrics to model-simulated trajectories from the annual recurrence map, treating the map output as the system time series from which the reference value and perturbation response are extracted. Reference state. For each system variable, the reference state  is defined as its value at the annual fixed point . Specifically, resistance is assessed using cumulative defoliation D as the system variable, with  (equilibrium defoliation); resilience is assessed using carrying capacity K as the system variable, with  (equilibrium carrying capacity). Different system variables may have different reference states, consistent with the multidimensional treatment of stability (Donohue et al., 2016; Hillebrand et al., 2018). Resistance is the capacity of the system to withstand change during a perturbation (Van Meerbeek et al., 2021). It is measured by the standardised index: (R1), where  is the value of the system variable at maximum deviation following a standardised pulse perturbation to the annual map (displacement of  by ± δ% of , with δ = 20% as the baseline perturbation magnitude). A resistance value of 1 indicates no deviation from the reference; 0 indicates deviation equal to the reference value. As complementary indicators, we also report the equilibrium cumulative defoliation D* and the peak within-season total larval density along the seasonal trajectory starting from fixed-point initial conditions. Resilience is the rate at which the system returns to its reference state after a perturbation (Van Meerbeek et al., 2021; Pimm, 1984). It is measured by the standardised index:  (R2), where  is the value of the system variable at maximum deviation from the reference (i.e.,  from the resistance measurement), and  is the value after Y annual iterations following the perturbation. We set Y = 5 annual cycles as the standardised recovery window, corresponding to a management-relevant planning horizon. A resilience value of 1 indicates full recovery within Y iterations; 0 indicates no net recovery. As complementary indicators, we report the dominant eigenvalue magnitude of the annual-map Jacobian at the fixed point and the characteristic return time , which captures asymptotic (linearised) resilience. Eigenvalue-based and trajectory-based resilience provide complementary information: the former reflects the local linear return rate, while the latter captures nonlinear dynamics far from the fixed point (Arnoldi et al., 2016; Medeiros et al., 2021). 
[P52] The two metric sets differ in their mathematical domain of validity. The eigenvalue-based indicators ρ* and  are properties of the Jacobian  evaluated at the fixed point x*; they characterise the linearised return rate and are therefore valid only for infinitesimally small perturbations within the immediate neighbourhood of x*. In contrast, R1 and R2 are computed from the full nonlinear annual map iterated from a finite-amplitude perturbation (±20% displacement of A_t). Because the Holling Type II functional responses in Equations 1-2 introduce curvature that the Jacobian cannot capture, the nonlinear trajectory may deviate substantially from the linearised prediction when the perturbation is large or when the system state approaches a basin boundary. Consequently, ρ* and R2 need not rank parameter scenarios identically: a fixed point with ρ* close to unity (slow linearised return) may nonetheless exhibit adequate finite-time recovery if the nonlinear restoring forces are stronger than the linearisation predicts, while a fixed point with small ρ* (fast linearised return) may yield low R2 if the perturbation places the trajectory near a separatrix between basins of attraction. Including both metric sets, therefore, ensures that stability assessment is not an artefact of the linearisation domain.
[P53] In a system with alternative stable states, a perturbation that pushes the system into a different basin of attraction will yield recovery near 0 or negative; a perturbation from which the system returns to x* yields recovery = 1. Latitude is the maximum perturbation magnitude the system can absorb before losing its ability to recover (Walker et al., 2004; Van Meerbeek et al., 2021). It is computed by progressively increasing the perturbation magnitude δ until the annual map fails to return to the original equilibrium within a specified horizon (50 annual iterations). The critical δ at which the system transitions to an alternative attractor defines the latitude boundary. This is related to the basin stability concept of Menck et al. (2013). Adaptive stability. We define adaptive stability as the capacity of the system to maintain ecological function through the joint action of within-season resistance and annual resilience, where the balance between resistance and resilience adapts to phenological conditions. Unlike engineering resilience (a single return-rate measure; Pimm, 1984), ecological resilience (a basin-of-attraction measure; Holling, 1973), or adaptive capacity (a social-ecological management concept; Folke et al., 2005), adaptive stability captures the multi-timescale interplay in which within-season resistance to outbreak damage and annual recovery of canopy condition jointly determine whether the system persists in a functional regime. The term is novel to this study; it is operationalised as the joint profile of resistance (R1) and resilience (R2) across phenological scenarios, rather than as a single scalar index. Three levels of stability loss. We distinguish three levels of stability loss that may or may not coincide (Van Meerbeek et al., 2021; Scheffer et al., 2001): (i) Loss of local asymptotic stability: the dominant eigenvalue magnitude ρ* crosses unity, and the annual fixed point loses its local attracting property. (ii) Functional threshold crossing: the system variable enters a functionally unacceptable range—specifically, the long-run mean defoliation exceeds  (corresponding to 50% seasonal defoliation; Ferretti et al., 2021), or the carrying capacity  falls below  for three or more consecutive years, indicating persistent canopy degradation, while potentially remaining in the same dynamic regime. (iii) Regime shift: the system transitions to a qualitatively different equilibrium (e.g., from coexistence to a parasitoid-free state), meaning the long-term dynamics converge to a structurally different attractor. The dynamical tipping boundary is the critical parameter value  at which  (Equation 19). The functional tipping criterion is evaluated relative to the dynamical boundary to determine whether ecological collapse precedes or follows the loss of mathematical stability.

[P55] 2.5 Numerical analyses
[P56] The numerical analyses are conducted across all nine phenological scenarios (Table S1), first by one-at-a-time parameter variation to isolate the effect of each scenario, and then by joint Latin Hypercube Sampling (JLHS) across all parameter ranges to capture interactions. This design enables the computation of equilibrium frequencies (O1), regime shift probabilities (O2), and strategy performance rankings (O3) across the full phenological parameter space.
[P57] The absence of long-term, site-specific time series for Beetle outbreak density, M. mutabilis parasitism prevalence, and passerine kill rates on this beetle challenges only numerical simulation-based analyses. Therefore, we focus on the system characteristic through mapping the structural stability landscape of the system and calibration for parameter ranges per regime. This technique allows us to identify parameter combinations and control allocations that support a stable low-damage equilibria: those that induce tipping, and the response of the parasitoid invasion threshold  to intraguild predation and control effort. However, this analysis therefore restricts us from aiming to predict site-specific outbreak timing or magnitude without site-specific time series data. Stability properties, including fixed-point existence, eigenvalue conditions, and bifurcation type, are determined by the structure of the annual map and the qualitative nature of the functional responses in our simulation for a range of plausible values based on stability analyses in annex 2 and known parameter values in annex 1. We follow this parameter range for simulation in the standardised protocol in theoretical eco-epidemic modelling, as it is in Chattopadhyay and Arino, 1999; Hethcote et al., 2004. 
[P58] During numerical analyses, parameters were divided into four classes: biological baseline parameters, calibrated structural parameters, bounded management controls, and objective-function weights. Biological parameters were assigned from empirical studies, cross-system proxies, or biologically constrained priors as summarised in Annex 1. Structural parameters lacking direct estimates but required for scale consistency or threshold behaviour, including , , and , were calibrated so that the model reproduced plausible baseline parasitism, daily bird predation, and proportional defoliation. The management variables , , and were not treated as ecological constants, but as bounded decision variables with baseline value zero. The weights in the objective functional were treated as scenario-dependent management preferences rather than empirically fixed ecological parameters.
[P59] To address the lack of precise parameter estimates, the analysis employs two complementary safeguards. First, all uncertain parameters are represented as intervals, as specified in the parameter tables of Annex 1, with empirically justified ranges. As a result, stability and control outcomes are reported as robust regions within parameter space rather than as point estimates. Conclusions are accepted only if they are valid across the entire range of each uncertain parameter. Second, structural robustness is assessed by replacing the baseline functional forms with ecologically plausible alternatives: Beverton-Holt versus Ricker recruitment in Equation (5), Holling Type II versus linear functional response for bird predation, and S → I compartmental parasitism versus direct parasitoid-induced mortality. If qualitative stability conclusions—specifically, which equilibrium is most probable (O1), whether the regime shift probability exceeds a policy-relevant threshold (O2), and which strategy is minimal-effort feasible (O3) remain consistent under both parametric and structural perturbations, these results are interpreted as intrinsic properties of the tri-trophic interaction architecture rather than artefacts of specific modelling assumptions.

[P93] Discussion
[P94] Climate-driven phenological variability alters both persistence and control within multitrophic systems (Renner & Zohner, 2018). This study models the Black alder–leaf beetle–parasitoid–generalist bird ecoepidemic system. The aim of this modelling-based, theory-driven study was threefold. It sought to identify the most probable stable equilibria across all phenological scenarios, quantify the likelihood of the system shifting from its current coexistence equilibrium to an undesirable state, and determine the minimal effort required for three pest control strategies targeting the leaf beetle to prevent regime shifts across parameter space. Our methodology involves process-based modelling. It employs the system's structural properties, gathered from well-known phenologies, and uses optimal control theory to address these objectives. Results indicate that the system does not inherently favour the most desirable equilibrium of coexistence. We analysed nine phenological scenarios and found that desirable coexistence, characterised by parasitoid persistence, low defoliation, and a maintained canopy, occurs only within a limited region of parameter space representing phenological variability. The probability of regime shifts increases substantially when phenological parameters vary simultaneously. Only the fully integrated management strategy is found to prioritise feasibility across both seasons and years. The model and analyses are calibrated within possible parameter ranges derived from literature on these phenologies across habitats that house our focal system. Thus, these results do not constitute stand-level forecasts for any specific alluvial forests. Instead, they provide a general understanding of the system's stability properties, including resilience and resistance dynamics, from an eco-epidemic point of view. The stability properties resulting from our methodology may be observed in part or in full across European habitats containing the focal system.
[P95] The primary ecological implication of Objective 1 is that considering parasitoids as the main natural control for phylophagous leaf beetle populations may compromise the stability of coexistence under phenological variability. Within the parameter space defined by phenological variability, both parasitoid-free and canopy-only states are more frequently observed as stable equilibria than coexistence. Coexistence remains possible only if the parasitoid attack rate on beetles increases, both host and parasitoid populations exhibit sufficient overwintering survival, and generalist birds exert moderate predation pressure. These observations are consistent with existing host–parasitoid models, which indicate that coexistence within Nicholson–Bailey frameworks is limited to a narrow parameter range (Singh, 2023). This result also supports findings that phenological mismatch can disrupt tritrophic stability, even when pairwise interactions remain stable (Belitz et al., 2025). The greater likelihood of parasitoid-free and canopy-only stable equilibria in this system across phenological variability provides important insight for distinguishing between the desirability of coexistence in complex systems and the likelihood of convergence to a simpler, yet stable, structure. Ecological complexity is often linked to increased productivity due to higher species diversity, making it a desirable system attribute. In this focal system, parasitoids help reduce the risk of future beetle outbreaks, while generalist birds have access to other, more abundant prey. These findings often motivate forest habitat managers to introduce additional parasitoids to control outbreaks, as the attack rate per parasitoid cannot be directly managed.
[P96] The ecological theory of intraguild predation provides a detailed framework for validating such management decisions. This is especially useful when direct empirical evidence is lacking and can be done through analogies with the present findings. This theory suggests a higher-order predator suppresses an intermediate natural enemy while still reducing the shared prey (Rosenheim, 1998; Polis & Holt, 1992). In our focal system, the precise sign and magnitude of bird predation on parasitised Beetle larvae remain unmeasured. Parasitised beetle larvae continue feeding until death induced by the parasitoid (Stireman et al., 2006). They remain susceptible to bird predation throughout the delay interval. Within the model, bird predation reduces the total larval pool and consequently lowers defoliation. However, it also removes parasitised larvae before the koinobiont parasitoid completes development and emerges. Each parasitised larva removed in this manner constitutes a lost recruitment event for the endoparasitoid. If the release of the parasite is targeted at the foraging aggregation of the beetles, the attack rate might increase. This is a signal the parasitoid tracks to find host beetle larvae (Stireman et al., 2006). We advise the central management question to be increasing both the abundance and the efficiency of natural enemies. In this context, coexistence loss is more accurately described as a failure of parasitoid attack rather than merely insufficient parasite number.
[P97] Objective 2 extends Objective 1 by examining how phenological variation changes the probability of leaving coexistence. It quantifies the probabilities of each phenological variation that can cause a regime shift across any stable equilibrium. Therefore, while the prediction of Objective 1 is limited to the initial state where the parasitoid is present, Objective 2 covers cases where the parasitoid is initially absent. A key observational difference is that a single parameter can affect two equilibria through different mechanisms. For example, season length does not visibly affect coexistence equilibria within the range studied, except by drastically increasing defoliation damage. However, with a parasitoid-free initial state, season length can destabilise coexistence. This occurs because increased defoliation reduces next year’s carrying capacity through a memory effect that reflects the accumulation of phytochemical defence that also changes the canopy as a feedback. Bird predation may reduce defoliation damage after a certain point. Its effect on coexistence is moderated by opposing intraguild predation effects if starting from the coexistence equilibrium. If starting from a parasitoid-free equilibrium, bird predation has no effect on parasitoid invasion risk. Phytochemical defence provides resistance and accumulates as plant memory, feeding back into the next year’s carrying capacity for both beetle and parasitoid. However, it has little influence on the invasion or equilibrium class. This observation is subject to further study, as quantification challenges make the accumulation rate and impact on next year’s growth poorly studied due to challenges in quantification. Since these results suggest that they are run on two different time scales, it establishes that resistance and resilience at each time scale shape stability differently in this system. Within-season trophic interactions determine parasitoid invasion risk. Year-to-year canopy defoliation memory of resistance determines the quality and persistence of long-term equilibrium. This supports a multi-metric stability framework (Van Meerbeek et al., 2021; Donohue et al., 2016). Invasion, resistance, and resilience capture different but complementary aspects of regime-shift risk. Consideration of examining resistance and resilience as two different stability metrics following the adaptive stability concept helped our understanding by examining the system's stability range via differential behaviour of these two metrics.  High Resistance across much of the coexistence regime implies that outbreak damage can stay low, while resilience varies more strongly as recovery capacity can still erode under phenological change. The system can therefore look stable in the short term, yet become dynamically fragile in the long term, as per our findings. Together, all the results of objective 2 show that stability failure can occur through multiple distinct routes. Management should therefore not infer security from low damage alone, but evaluate invasion potential, recovery rate, and attractor-switch risk simultaneously. 
[P98] In this study, we used Eigenvalue-based and trajectory-based metrics to represent mechanistically distinct aspects of stability. Within the Latin Hypercube sample, their correlation is positive but not perfect. Certain parameter sets demonstrate that the eigenvalue indicates rapid return, while trajectory-based resilience remains low due to finite perturbations nearing the basin boundary, and the reverse can also occur. From an ecological perspective, eigenvalue-based resilience characterises recovery from routine interannual fluctuations, whereas trajectory-based resilience characterises recovery from discrete, large-amplitude disturbances. This distinction similarly separates the equilibrium damage margin from the transient resistance index. Reporting both metrics is essential for distinguishing chronic degradation risk from acute perturbation risk, which is directly relevant for selecting between sustained and emergency management strategies. In addition to the probability of regime shift under this adaptive stability, objective 2 aims to identify potential early warning signals using three joint criteria. First, the parameter must contribute substantially to regime-shift probability. Second, it must show a partial rank correlation with the eigenvalue that indicates a destabilising influence. Third, the associated phenological process must be empirically observable during a simulation experiment searching for system destabilisation. The candidate phenologies and their association with other phenologies as early warning signals, identified under Objective 2, are proposed as ranked monitoring priorities. They are not validated early warning signals capable of reliably indicating impending regime shifts, since our parameters come from multiple habitats. The three conditions set for early warning signal identification are not interchangeable during system analyses. For example, a parameter may drive eigenvalue  toward the stability boundary without greatly increasing regime-shift probability. Alternatively, a parameter may contribute to regime shifts but not be observable in practice. Among the identified candidates, season length is the most tractable. It can be measured with growing degree days or satellite-derived phenology. In contrast, directly estimating parasitoid attack rate or adult overwintering requires intensive field sampling. This has not yet been demonstrated at management-relevant scales in many systems (Nimalrathna et al., 2022). These variables provide a foundation for targeted monitoring designs and future studies on early warning signals of this system through phenologies.
[P99] The management implication of this study comes from the optimal theory-based result in Objective 3. The result suggests that it is not simply that one strategy reduced beetle damage faster than the others. Under the study’s decision rule, a strategy is compared on cost only after it first proves ecologically feasible. In practical terms, this means the strategy must keep seasonal canopy loss below the critical damage level. It must also prevent the alder stand from slipping into a persistently degraded low-canopy condition and maintain a long-term managed state. In this state, parasitoids must remain present, and control must continue after small disturbances. All three strategies reduced canopy damage below the critical level within the first year. However, only the fully integrated strategy met those broader conditions. This strategy combined targeted larval removal, parasitoid release, and bird-habitat enhancement. The parasitoid-only strategy and the parasitoid-plus-bird strategy both improved short-term suppression but did not produce a self-maintaining low-damage state in the long run. This distinction separates temporary outbreak suppression from durable restoration. The key result is that the integrated strategy was not only the least costly, but was also the only one that reduced defoliation, supported canopy recovery, and preserved stable parasitoid-supported biological control. Cost ranking becomes meaningful only after the feasibility filter is passed. The ecological meanings used here follow your annex definitions of cumulative canopy damage, canopy availability, targeted larval removal, parasitoid release, and bird-habitat enhancement. 
[P100] Our results from objective 3 also suggest that intervention timing matters for optimising the effort of the intervention. Early action prevents current-season beetle damage from being carried over into the next year as a structural canopy penalty. In this ecoepidemic system, reducing larval density early in the outbreak lowers the total defoliation accumulated over the season. This weakens the foliage-memory feedback. Heavy canopy loss otherwise reduces the forest’s effective canopy support in the following year. As a result, the stand enters the next season in better condition. The amount of control needed later is reduced. All three management strategies, therefore, concentrate most of their effort at the beginning of the intervention period. Their efforts decline thereafter as the system moves toward a lower-damage managed state. Ecologically, this means the system responds best when control is applied before repeated feeding damage has accumulated enough to degrade future canopy recovery. The exact schedule may differ under alternative recovery assumptions, climate forcing, or cost structures. However, the general advantage of early, front-loaded action appears robust in this framework.
[P101] Our study integrates processes often studied separately, such as seasonal beetle feeding, delayed parasitoid development, bird predation on both healthy and parasitised larvae, interannual canopy damage, and diverse pest management. The main contribution is a comprehensive framework using a systems ecology view. This framework fills a literature gap on the system’s structure-dependent multiple metrics that define adaptive stability properties. From a modelling perspective, our study contributes by integrating an eco-epidemiological system that spans multiple trophic levels and shows intraguild predation. It also integrates optimal control theory through habitat management strategies as intervention measures. Most host-parasitoid models have focused just on insect roles and often exclude plant feedback or higher-order predators since the past century (Ji, Vasilyeva, Mbroh, & Sadovski, 2025; Nicholson & Bailey, 1935; Hassell et al., 1976). Many plant defence models look at how herbivory carries over across years (Hakes & Cronin, 2012), but they rarely assess management strategies. In addition to bridging these modelling gaps, our study also connects seasonal and annual phenology in a single model. This unification enables analysis of immediate damage control, long-term recovery, regime shifts, and explores the most effective management options under optimal control theory. However, as described in our methods, annex, and early warning signal discussion, varied sources for parameters limit our results to a general outline. This restricts us from implementing in a specific habitat directly without empirical data from that habitat. With long-term field data on beetle outbreaks, parasitoid numbers, bird predation, and other life-cycle stages from one habitat, our model could pinpoint regime shift chances, coexistence equilibrium, and disease-free equilibrium more precisely. Such data would also test the early warning signals and links we identified in our results. As mentioned earlier, our analysis is supported by systems structure using well-studied phenologies and their variability across space and time to map adaptive stability. Theories on adaptive stability and intraguild predation back up the findings (Van Meerbeek et al., 2021; Polis & Holt, 1992). Empirical, habitat-specific data for each habitat can determine (1) how habitat-specific parameter values narrow stability ranges for this system, and (2) help map the distribution of those habitats across the stability plane to inform further prioritisation of habitats at risk of regime shift. Therefore, this study aims to connect what we know about black alder phenology differences across habitats with future studies on spatial variability in adaptive stability of this system across black alder-rich habitats.

[P184] Annex 2: Stability Analyses
[P185] This annex presents the complete mathematical framework for the stability analysis of the hybrid seasonal–annual eco-epidemic model described in the main text (Equations 1-8). The long-term stability of this system is determined by the annual recurrence map rather than by the within-season ordinary differential equation (ODE) considered in isolation. The within-season ODE (Equations 1-4) characterises loss and accumulation processes over a finite interval τ ∈ [0, T]. Setting  produces within-season pseudo-equilibria, which do not represent ecologically relevant steady states, since the system is reset annually through recruitment, overwinter survival, and foliage recovery equations (Equations 5-8). Multi-year dynamic-including outbreak recurrence, parasitoid persistence, and tipping risk,are governed by the annual map.
[P186] The stability analysis is analytically well-posed but does not admit a closed-form solution. Specifically, the annual map G and its structure, existence conditions for fixed points, the Jacobian  of the annual map (derived via the chain rule and variational equations), the parasitoid invasion threshold RP as an eigenvalue of JG, local stability criteria (spectral radius less than one), and bifurcation conditions (eigenvalue crossing the unit circle) are all derived analytically. In contrast, the seasonal flow ΦT and its derivatives are evaluated numerically, as the Holling Type II functional responses render the within-season ODE nonlinear and analytically intractable. This approach, combining analytic stability conditions with numerical evaluation of the seasonal flow, is standard and mathematically rigorous for hybrid eco-epidemic models (Kot, 2001; Kuznetsov, 2004).The analysis proceeds through a ten-step framework, each anchored to the specific theorem, criterion, or mathematical principle that it employs.

[P213] Step 3: Identification of fixed points (annual equilibria) of the map
[P214] Fixed points are obtained by solving the nonlinear system  numerically via Newton iteration or continuation methods (Allgower and Georg, 2003). The following ecologically distinct fixed points are sought.
[P215] (i) Trivial (extinction) fixed point: Trivial (extinction) fixed point x₀* = (0, 0, K₀): A* = 0, F* = 0, K* = K₀ This corresponds to the beetle-free state. When A = 0, no larvae are present, so (parasitoid decays without hosts), and D(T) = 0. Hence This fixed point is relevant only as a boundary reference.
[P216] (ii) Parasitoid-free fixed point x₁* = (A*, 0, K*): The beetle persists at a density regulated by bird predation, baseline mortality, and foliage feedback, but no parasitoid is present (F* = 0). When F = 0, the parasitism term βFS/(1 + hS) vanishes, so I(τ) ≡ 0 throughout the season. The susceptible larvae decay under natural mortality and bird predation only, and At+1 = σA S(T). The self-consistency condition A* = σA S(T; A*, 0, K*) uniquely determines A* for given K* and Bt because the Beverton–Holt function S(0) = RBA/(1 + A/K) is monotonically increasing and concave in A (Beverton and Holt, 1957), and the subsequent within-season decay is monotonically decreasing. This is the state from which parasitoid invasion is assessed.
[P217] (iii) Coexistence (endemic parasitism) fixed point x₂* = (A*, F*, K*) with F* > 0: Both the beetle and the parasitoid persist, and the parasitoid exerts ongoing biological control. This is the management target. At this fixed point, the parasitoid population is sustained by sufficient susceptible host density to overcome within-season mortality and overwinter attrition.
[P218] Existence conditions for each fixed point are derived implicitly,, the terminal states ST, FT, DT depend on x* through ΦT, so closed-form expressions are generally unavailable due to the nonlinearity of the Holling Type II terms. The existence and number of fixed points are determined by Newton iteration with warm-start initialisation (iterating the map forward ~100–200 years to approach the attractor, then refining with Newton’s method), and their dependence on parameters can be tracked via numerical continuation (Allgower and Georg, 2003).
[P219] Step 4: Local stability of annual fixed points via the Jacobian of the annual map.
[P220] Criterion used: Spectral radius criterion for discrete-time maps.
[P221] Theorem (Elaydi, 2005, Theorem 4.15): A fixed point x* of a discrete-time map xt+1 = G(xt) is locally asymptotically stable if and only if all eigenvalues of the Jacobian matrix JG(x*) = ∂G/∂x|x* lie strictly inside the unit circle in the complex plane, i.e., maxi |λi| < 1, where λi are the eigenvalues of JG(x*).
[P222] Justification: Discrete-time Hartman–Grobman linearisation theorem.
[P223] Theorem (Kuznetsov, 2004, Chapter 2; Palis and de Melo, 1982): Near a hyperbolic fixed point (no eigenvalues on the unit circle), the nonlinear map G and its linearisation x → JG(x*)(x − x*) are topologically conjugate, i.e., there exists a homeomorphism that maps orbits of one to orbits of the other, preserving orientation. This guarantees that the local qualitative dynamics (stability, instability, saddle structure) of the nonlinear map are completely determined by the eigenvalues of JG(x*).
[P224] Important distinction: This is the correct stability test for the annual recurrence. The Routh–Hurwitz criterion (Re(λi) < 0) applies to continuous-time systems and would be appropriate only for the within-season ODE in isolation. For the discrete-time annual map, the unit circle (not the imaginary axis) is the stability boundary.

[P276] Stability implications.
[P277] The parasitoid-free fixed point x₁* is locally asymptotically stable as an annual equilibrium if and only if (a) RP < 1, and (b) the beetle–foliage eigenvalues of the resident subsystem also lie inside the unit circle. At RP = 1, a transcritical bifurcation occurs: the coexistence fixed point x₂* becomes biologically feasible (F* > 0) and, generically, inherits stability for RP > 1 while x₁* loses stability in the parasitoid direction.

[P281] Step 7: Identification of tipping points as bifurcation boundaries of the annual map
[P282] The operational definitions of resistance, resilience, adaptive stability, tipping, and the strategy comparison protocol are given in Section 2.2 of the main text; Annex 2 provides the mathematical derivations underlying those quantities.
[P283] Criterion used: Bifurcation theory for discrete-time maps.
[P284] Theorem (Kuznetsov, 2004, Chapter 4): A fixed point x*(p) of a parametrised discrete-time map xt+1 = G(xt; p) undergoes a bifurcation at p = pc when an eigenvalue (or a pair of eigenvalues) of JG(x*(p)) crosses the unit circle. The generic codimension-one bifurcations of discrete-time maps are: (a) saddle-node (fold), (b) period-doubling (flip), and (c) Neimark–Sacker (discrete Hopf).
[P285] Application.
[P286] A tipping parameter p (chosen from {RB, ϕ, cB, β, uP,max, uB,max} according to the management question) is varied, and the eigenvalues λi(p) of JG(x*(p)) are tracked. The regulated (low-damage) fixed point x*(p) is locally asymptotically stable as long as maxi |λi(p)| < 1. A tipping boundary occurs at the critical value p = pc where:
[P287] maxᵢ |λᵢ(pᴄ)| = 1	(19)
[P288] Classification of tipping types.
[P289] (a) Saddle-node (fold) tipping: A real eigenvalue crosses +1. This corresponds to the collision and disappearance of the low-damage and high-damage fixed points. Ecologically, the stable low-damage equilibrium ceases to exist and the system collapses abruptly to a high-damage state.
[P290] (b) Flip (period-doubling) tipping: A real eigenvalue crosses −1. This corresponds to the onset of biennial outbreak cycling. Ecologically, this produces alternating outbreak and recovery years—the system oscillates between high-damage and low-damage states with period 2.
[P291] (c) Neimark–Sacker (discrete Hopf) tipping: A complex conjugate pair of eigenvalues crosses the unit circle. This corresponds to the onset of quasi-periodic outbreak oscillations. Ecologically, this produces irregular multi-year outbreak cycles whose period is generally incommensurate with the annual timescale.
[P292] Functional tipping criteria.
[P293] In addition to the dynamical tipping boundary (Equation 19), a functional tipping criterion is defined to capture ecological collapse that may not coincide exactly with the mathematical bifurcation. Two functional thresholds are monitored: (i) the carrying capacity Kt falls below a regeneration threshold Kmin for m ≥ 3 consecutive years, indicating persistent canopy degradation; or (ii) the long-run mean damage exceeds a critical threshold Dcrit. The functional tipping boundary is located relative to the dynamical bifurcation boundary to assess whether ecological collapse precedes or follows the loss of mathematical stability.
[P294] Multistability and basin boundaries.
[P295] For parameter values near pc, the annual map is iterated forward from multiple initial conditions to detect multistability—the coexistence of a low-damage attractor and a high-damage attractor (or stable cycle) separated by a basin boundary. If multistability exists, tipping can occur by perturbation (basin crossing) even before the dynamical bifurcation, and the relative basin sizes are reported as a measure of tipping risk.

[P297] Step 8: Optimal control via Pontryagin’s Maximum Principle and the forward–backward sweep method
[P298] Purpose.
[P299] Determine the optimal allocation of within-season control effort (parasitoid augmentation uP and targeted larval removal uC) that minimises the combined ecological and economic cost.
[P300] Theorem used: Pontryagin’s Maximum Principle (PMP).
[P301] Theorem (Pontryagin et al., 1962; Fleming and Rishel, 1975): For a control system y′ = f(y, u) with cost functional J = ∫ L(y, u) dτ + Ψ(y(T)), there exist costate variables λ(τ) such that, at the optimum, the Hamiltonian H = L + λᵀf satisfies: (a) the state equations y′ = ∂H/∂λ = f; (b) the costate equations λ′ = −∂H/∂y; (c) the stationarity condition ∂H/∂u = 0 at the optimal control; and (d) the transversality condition λ(T) = ∂Ψ/∂y(T).
[P302] Sign convention (minimisation).
[P303] Convention adopted: The Hamiltonian is defined in the standard minimisation form H = L + λᵀf, and the optimal control minimises H. This convention, used throughout Lenhart and Workman (2007) and Fleming and Rishel (1975), determines the signs of all subsequent control characterisations. The costate equations are λ′ = −∂H/∂y, and the transversality conditions are λ(T) = ∂Ψ/∂y(T).

[P324] Numerical solution: Forward–backward sweep method (FBSM).
[P325] The resulting two-point boundary value problem is solved numerically using the forward–backward sweep method (FBSM), which iterates until convergence:
[P326] (i) Forward integration of the state system (Equations 1–4) with current control estimates from τ = 0 to τ = T.
[P327] (ii) Backward integration of the adjoint system (dλ/dτ = −∂H/∂y) from τ = T to τ = 0 using the terminal transversality conditions.
[P328] (iii) Update of the controls via Equations (21- (22), with convex combination damping: unew = α uupdate + (1 − α) uold (with α = 0.5).
[P329] (iv) Convergence check: repeat until max|Δu| < ε (tolerance ε = 10⁻⁴).
[P330] Convergence of the FBSM for systems of this structure is established by Hackbusch (1978) and applied in eco-epidemic contexts by Lenhart and Workman (2007, Chapter 11). The FBSM yields the optimal within-season profiles u*P(τ) and u*C(τ) for a single season. In the multi-year setting, the FBSM is applied sequentially within each year (receding-horizon / myopic strategy): the annual state xt determines the initial conditions, the FBSM computes the optimal within-season controls, and the resulting terminal values determine xt+1 via the annual map. This sequential approach is distinct from solving the full N-year dynamic programming problem simultaneously, and the text reports results accordingly.

[P336] Control scenarios for hypothesis testing.
[P337] Scenario A: uP > 0, uB = 0, uC = 0 (parasitoid augmentation only). Tests whether parasitoid attack alone is sufficient for system stability (H₁ₐ).
[P338] Scenario B: uP > 0, uB > 0, uC = 0 (combined parasitoid and bird-habitat enhancement). Tests whether bird predation provides essential complementary resistance (H₁ᵇ).
[P339] Scenario C: uP > 0, uB > 0, uC > 0 (full integrated control). Benchmark for the maximum achievable stabilisation.
[P340] Performance criteria.
[P341] Control performance is evaluated by four criteria applied to each scenario's long-run trajectory: (a) whether the equilibrium defoliation D* remains below D_crit = 0.5; (b) whether the equilibrium carrying capacity K* remains above K_min = 0.5 K₀; (c) the dominant eigenvalue magnitude ρ* at the controlled fixed point (resilience); and (d) the total multi-year cost J. Additionally, the Van Meerbeek resistance (R1) and resilience (R2) indices are computed for each scenario at the controlled fixed point.
[P342] Strategy decision rule. Strategies are compared using a lexicographic decision rule: (i) feasibility-only strategies satisfying D* < D_crit and K* > K_min and maintaining parasitoid coexistence (F* > 0) are considered; (ii) among feasible strategies, the one with minimum total cost J is selected as the minimal-effort strategy for each phenological scenario. H1 is supported if the most probable equilibrium across parameter space is the desirable coexistence equilibrium. If it is not, the analysis reports which phenological scenarios prevent convergence to the desirable state.
[P343] Purpose.
[P344] Assess whether the qualitative stability conclusions and hypothesis decisions are robust to uncertainty in parameter values and model structure.
[P345] Method 1: Latin Hypercube Sampling with Partial Rank Correlation Coefficients (LHS–PRCC).
[P346] Framework (Marino et al., 2008; Saltelli et al., 2008): Because several parameters are anchored by cross-system proxies rather than direct field measurements for the Beetle–M. mutabilis system (notably β, h, σA, σF), all stability and optimal control results are evaluated for robustness across the full plausible parameter ranges reported in the parameter tables (Sections 2.1.2–2.1.3 of the main text). Uncertain parameters are treated as intervals, and the analysis reports robust stability regions in parameter space—regions where the annual fixed point remains stable (all eigenvalues inside the unit circle) for all sampled parameter combinations.
[P347] Sensitivity of RP, the tipping boundary pc, and the optimal cost J to each parameter is quantified using partial rank correlation coefficients (PRCC) computed from Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS). LHS stratifies each parameter’s range into N equal-probability intervals and samples one value per interval with random permutation across parameters, ensuring uniform marginal coverage. PRCC then computes the correlation between each parameter and the output after removing the linear effects of all other parameters via rank regression, yielding a measure of monotonic sensitivity that is valid for nonlinear but monotonic input–output relationships.
[P348] A qualitative stability conclusion (i.e., which equilibrium is most probable, the regime shift probability, and the minimal-effort strategy ranking) is considered robust if it does not change across the full plausible parameter space.
[P349] Method 2: Structural robustness via alternative functional forms.
[P350] In addition to parametric sensitivity, structural robustness is assessed by comparing the baseline model against alternative functional form assumptions:
[P351] (i) Beverton–Holt versus Ricker recruitment in Equation (5): S(0) = RBA/(1 + A/K) versus S(0) = A exp(r(1 − A/K)). This tests sensitivity to the form of density-dependent recruitment.
[P352] (ii) Holling Type II versus linear functional response for bird predation: cBBtS/(1 + aB(S + I)) versus cBBtS. This tests sensitivity to the assumed saturation in bird foraging.
[P353] (iii) S → I compartmental parasitism versus direct parasitoid-induced mortality: the current model (parasitised larvae continue feeding until parasitoid emergence) versus a model where parasitism directly removes larvae. This tests whether the explicit infected class is necessary for the qualitative conclusions.
[P354] If the qualitative stability conclusions (i.e., which hypothesis is supported and which bifurcation type governs tipping) remain unchanged under both parametric and structural perturbations, they are considered to reflect genuine properties of the tri-trophic interaction architecture rather than artefacts of specific modelling choices.

[P356] Model Adequacy and Data-Lean Design
[P357] The lack of long-term, site-specific time series for Beetle outbreak density, M. mutabilis parasitism prevalence, and passerine kill rates on this beetle does not constrain the class of results targeted by this study. The primary contribution of the model is to map the structural stability landscape by identifying parameter combinations and control allocations that yield a stable low-damage equilibrium, those that result in tipping, and the response of the parasitoid invasion threshold RP to intraguild predation and control effort.
[P358] The focus is on structural stability properties (fixed-point existence, eigenvalue conditions, bifurcation type), which depend on the architecture of the annual map and the qualitative form of the functional responses, rather than on precise numerical parameter values. This approach aligns with standard practice in theoretical eco-epidemic modelling (Chattopadhyay and Arino, 1999; Hethcote et al., 2004), where the mathematical framework is constructed to be valid across the plausible parameter space.
[P359] To address the absence of precise parameter estimates, the analysis incorporates two complementary safeguards. First, all uncertain parameters are treated as intervals, as reported in the parameter tables of Sections 2.1.2–2.1.3 with empirically justified ranges, and stability and control results are presented as robust regions in parameter space rather than as point estimates. Conclusions are accepted only if they hold across the entire range of each uncertain parameter. Second, structural robustness is evaluated by substituting the baseline functional forms with ecologically plausible alternatives (Step 10). If the qualitative stability conclusions remain unchanged under both parametric and structural perturbations, they are considered to reflect genuine properties of the tri-trophic interaction architecture rather than artefacts of specific modelling choices.
